```yaml
slug: tax-estimator

hub: freelancer
lastUpdated: 2026-03-26
keyTakeaways:
  - "Tax brackets are marginal -- only the income within each bracket is taxed at that rate, not your entire income"
  - "The standard deduction ($15,000+ for single filers in 2026) means your first dollars of income are tax-free"
  - "Your effective tax rate is always lower than your marginal tax bracket"
faqs:
  - question: "What is the difference between marginal and effective tax rate?"
    answer: "Your marginal rate is the bracket your last dollar of income falls into. Your effective rate is the actual percentage of total income you pay in taxes. Because of progressive brackets, your effective rate is always lower than your marginal rate."
  - question: "Should I itemize or take the standard deduction?"
    answer: "Take whichever is larger. Most people benefit from the standard deduction since it increased significantly in recent years. Itemizing makes sense if your mortgage interest, state taxes, and charitable donations exceed the standard deduction."
  - question: "How do tax brackets work for married couples?"
    answer: "Married filing jointly has wider brackets, meaning more income is taxed at lower rates. The brackets are roughly double the single filer brackets for the first several tiers, reducing the 'marriage penalty' for most couples."
  - question: "Do I need to pay estimated taxes as a freelancer?"
    answer: "Yes, if you expect to owe $1,000 or more in taxes. The IRS expects quarterly payments (April 15, June 15, September 15, January 15). Underpaying can trigger penalties."
relatedGuides:
  - budget
  - retirement
```

Content sections: How Tax Brackets Actually Work (<Term>Tax Bracket</Term>, <Term>Filing Status</Term> -- destroy the "if I earn more I'll take home less" myth), Understanding Deductions (<Term>Deduction</Term>, standard vs itemized), Tax Credits vs Deductions (key distinction), Your Effective vs Marginal Rate (worked example with $75,000 salary), Self-Employment Tax Basics (<Term>Gross Income</Term>), Common Tax Mistakes, Planning Ahead: How to Owe Less Next Year.
